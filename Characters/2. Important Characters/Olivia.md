### Main Backstory
Jason was nine when the collapse began, old enough to understand that the world was ending but too young to accept it. He lived in Chicago with his grandmother, Lily, after his parents died in a car accident when he was five. Lily was a retired librarian who filled their apartment with books and stories, teaching Jason that knowledge was the one thing that could never be taken away.

When the first waves of climate disasters hit Chicago, Lily and Jason joined a community group trying to preserve books and knowledge. They spent months microfilming documents, digitizing records, and creating seed libraries. Lily believed that when humanity finally healed, they would need the accumulated wisdom of the past to build something better.

At twelve, Jason watched Lily succumb to the plague that swept through the refugee camps. Before she died, she made him promise to "remember the good things, protect the innocent, and never let hatred make you into something she wouldn't recognize." She pressed her father's compass into his hands and told him it would always point him toward home, even when home was just an idea.

Jason spent his teenage years in various survivor communities around the Great Lakes, using his grandmother's compass to navigate not just geography but moral terrain. He apprenticed with Marcus Washington, a former Detroit police officer who taught him that strength without compassion was just another form of brutality. Marcus showed him how to fight, but more importantly, when not to.

At seventeen, Jason's community was overrun by the Iron Brotherhood, a militant group that enslaved anyone they deemed "useful." Jason spent two years as a forced laborer, building fortifications and watching the Brotherhood's casual cruelty. He eventually escaped during a winter storm, but not before helping twelve other prisoners flee, including a pregnant woman he carried for miles through the snow.

He spent his early twenties moving westward, searching for the rumored safe zones beyond the Rockies. He found instead a continent full of small communities struggling to maintain their humanity against impossible odds. He worked as a medic, a teacher, a guard, whatever was needed. He saved a village's children from contaminated water in Wyoming, helped rebuild a school in Utah, and held dying strangers who just needed someone to witness their final moments.

Five years ago, Jason was part of a group that tried to establish a permanent settlement in western Colorado. For eighteen months, they built something beautiful: gardens, workshops, a small library, and most importantly, trust. Then came the drought, the crop failures, and the difficult decisions about who would eat and who would starve. Jason watched good people turn against each other, saw families torn apart by desperation, and learned that sometimes the greatest act of love is knowing when to leave.

He's spent the last three years wandering alone, carrying Lily's compass and the weight of every person he couldn't save. Despite everything, he still stops to help strangers, still shares his food with the hungry, and still believes that somewhere beyond the wasteland, humans are building something worth finding.
