## The Road to Ashenfall

Three weeks had passed since the storm brought them together, and Raven had learned that Jason hummed old songs under his breath when he thought she wasn't listening. Classical pieces, lullabies, fragments of a world that had believed music would outlast concrete. She found it oddly comforting, this proof that somewhere beneath the layers of survival and sorrow, beauty still lived in him.

They had been walking east, following rumors of a trade settlement called Haven's Rest, when they encountered the refugees. A family of four, walking west with the hollow-eyed determination of people fleeing something unspeakable. The father's arm hung useless at his side, poorly bandaged and smelling of infection. The mother carried a child who hadn't spoken since they'd met her.

"Don't go to [[Ashenfall]]," the woman had whispered when Raven offered her water. "The city is cursed. The dead won't stay buried there."

Jason had knelt beside the injured man, examining the wound with the gentle efficiency Raven had come to admire. "What happened to you?"

"The towers," the man gasped, fever bright in his eyes. "They're falling, one by one. But they don't fall clean. They lean, they groan, they drop pieces like tears. We lived on the fifteenth floor of the [[Meridian Building]]. Thought we were safe up high, away from the gangs and the rising water. Then the building started to tilt."

His wife took up the story, her voice mechanical with trauma. "We had maybe an hour to get out before it would crush the building next to it. But the stairwells were flooded. The elevators died twenty years ago. We had to climb down the outside, using cables and ropes. People fell. We heard them screaming all the way down."

The child in her arms finally spoke, a whisper that chilled Raven's blood: "The buildings are hungry, mama. They eat people when they fall down."

Now, two days later, Raven and Jason stood on a hill overlooking the city of [[Ashenfall]], and the child's words echoed in both their minds. The metropolis spread before them like a mouth full of broken teeth, fifty square miles of urban decay slowly drowning in its own tears.

The name had once been Ashland Falls, a proud industrial city built around a series of waterfalls that had powered factories and mills. Now the falls ran black with chemical runoff, and the city had earned its new name from the ash that fell like snow from the perpetually burning ruins in the financial district.

Water was everywhere and nowhere. The river had jumped its banks decades ago, turning the lower city into a poisonous lagoon where the tops of cars and street signs broke the surface like the fins of metallic sharks. But higher up, where they needed to go to reach the eastern districts, the old water treatment plants had failed, leaving entire neighborhoods dying of thirst while standing in the shadow of flooded streets.

"Jesus," Jason breathed, and Raven heard twenty-five years of accumulated horrors in that single word.

The skyscrapers dominated the skyline like a gathering of giants in various states of collapse. Some stood relatively straight, their windows blown out and walls streaked with decades of rain and rust. Others leaned at impossible angles, held up only by the buildings they pressed against, creating archways of impending disaster. The [[Meridian Building]], which the refugee family had fled, tilted at a forty-five-degree angle against the old courthouse, both structures locked in a slow-motion dance of mutual destruction.

But it was the sounds that made Raven's skin crawl. Even from three miles away, they could hear it: a constant groaning, like the city itself was in pain. Metal stressed beyond its limits, concrete cracking under impossible weight, and underneath it all, a rhythmic splashing that might have been waves against submerged buildings or something much worse moving through the flooded streets.



Raven pulled out her binoculars, salvaged from a dead park ranger two months back, and studied the city more closely. What she saw made her stomach turn.

Bodies hung from some of the tilted buildings, not executed but trapped when the structures began their slow collapse. Families who had tried to escape through windows only to find themselves suspended in a web of power lines and twisted metal, left to die of thirst and exposure while the city groaned its funeral song around them. The bodies swayed in the breeze, mummified by the dry air and chemical rain, their clothes serving as prayer flags for a god that had abandoned this place long ago.

"There," she said, handing Jason the binoculars and pointing to a route that zigzagged between the most stable-looking buildings. "If we stay on the elevated roadway, we can avoid the worst of the flooding. But we'll have to move fast. Some of those buildings look like they could come down any minute."

Jason studied the path she'd indicated. The elevated highway was partially collapsed in places, leaving gaps they'd have to jump or climb around. But it was their best chance of avoiding both the poisoned water below and the more unstable structures.

"What about the enemies?" he asked. They'd heard stories of the gangs that controlled different sections of Ashenfall, territorial as sharks and twice as vicious.

"The Tide Runners control the flooded district," Raven said, reciting intelligence gathered from other travelers. "They move through the water like it's land, using boats and makeshift bridges. But they rarely go up high because of the falling debris."

She pointed to the mid-level buildings, those six to twelve stories tall that formed a concrete maze between the skyscrapers and the flood. "The Rust Eaters have the middle ground. They're scavengers, mostly, but they'll kill for good salvage. And up in the towers..." She trailed off, studying the highest buildings through the binoculars.

"What about the towers?"

"Nobody knows. People go up there sometimes, looking for a better view of the eastern districts. But they don't come back down. At least, not the same way they went up."

Jason took the binoculars back and focused on one of the leaning skyscrapers. Through a broken window on the thirty-second floor, he could see movement. Something pale and jerky, like a marionette with half its strings cut. As he watched, the figure pressed against the glass, and for a moment he thought he could see its face: human once, but stretched and wrong, as if the years of living in a slowly collapsing building had reshaped it into something else entirely.

"We go fast, we go quiet, and we don't look up," he said, handing back the binoculars.

They began their descent toward the city as the sun reached its zenith, casting sharp shadows that would provide some concealment. The closer they got, the worse it smelled: stagnant water, rotting vegetation, and underneath it all, the sweet-sick smell of old death that no amount of rain could wash away.

The first body they encountered was crucified on a stop sign that jutted up from the flooded street like a periscope. The message was clear: this territory belonged to someone, and trespassers would be dealt with accordingly. The corpse wore the remnants of a military uniform, though whether from before the collapse or some post-apocalyptic militia was impossible to tell.

They reached the base of the elevated highway just as something large moved through the water fifty yards to their left. Whatever it was created a wake like a small boat, but moved with the sinuous motion of a living thing. Raven caught a glimpse of scales or possibly metal before it disappeared beneath the murky surface.

"The water's not just poisoned," she whispered as they climbed the collapsed concrete ramp to the highway level. "Something's living in it."

The elevated roadway stretched before them like a concrete ribbon threading through a forest of dying giants. To their left, the Crimson Tower leaned at such an angle that rain ran up its windows instead of down. To their right, the old city hall had partially collapsed onto the building next to it, creating a bridge of rubble and rebar that looked like a prehistoric creature's spine.

But it was ahead of them that made both travelers stop and stare in horror. The highway passed directly beneath the Weeping Sisters, two identical office buildings that had been slowly collapsing toward each other for years. They now formed a precarious arch, held apart only by the tangled mass of debris, furniture, and human remains that had fallen into the gap between them. Water dripped constantly from this grotesque collection, hence the buildings' nickname, but Raven suspected the liquid falling on the highway below wasn't entirely rainwater.

"We go under fast," Jason said, unconsciously lowering his voice. "Don't look up, don't stop, and whatever you do, don't listen to anything you might hear from inside those buildings."

As they approached the shadow of the Weeping Sisters, the sound grew louder: not just the groan of stressed metal and concrete, but something else. Something that might have been voices, if voices could survive being crushed and stretched and left to echo in a maze of twisted steel for twenty-five years.

They ran.

Behind them, as they emerged from the other side of the arch, something fell from the gap between the buildings and hit the highway with a wet splat. Neither of them looked back to see what it was.
